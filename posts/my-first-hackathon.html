It was a random call by my friend which led me into trying the hardware hackathon.

Spoiler: We built a bionic arm.

Pre-Story

Not the hardware part but as a whole, a hardware hackathon to me was really the first one I had attended. It was on a tight schedule because it was really in the midst of my internals — but that's out of topic. So, let us let it be.

Technical Part

So, for the motor drivers and the arduino part — this is what I understood and could explain.

Arduinos are a variety of MCU. The one provided to us — the Nano — works at 5V as its supply. It usually works with power supplied via USB connected to an external device while flashing.

For our circuitry, it works in the following way if simplified while sending the signals from the arduino to the motors:

(nano -> m-driver -> voltage-pins -> motors)

Signal Types

  • Analog Signals: Analog Signals are the continuous signals.
  • Digital Signals: Digital Signals are discrete value containing signals.
  • PWM (Pulse Width Modulation): The process of modulation of a wave such that the wave is made a single way, by the use of digital signals.

So, we used the PWM supporting pins in the Arduino Nano to control the speed of the motor using the enable A and enable B pins in the motor driver. The remaining 4 pins were utilized in supplying digital signal without PWM, which solely sent data to either rotate or not rotate the motor.

The code was pretty easy as it was a black box and we only had to use the Arduino IDE to flash and compile. It just required setting up all the required data in the setup() function and all the necessary data in the loop() function. The most important thing I learnt about programming arduino was that there shouldn't be much or any use of loops because they interfere with the workings of the hardware and don't supply enough resource to the components. So, always use an if statement.
